Towards a Unified Model of Musiâ€‹cal Form

  Tonal music has evolved a range of organized structures such that a listening mind can interpret it. Musical form involves segmentation, grouping, repetition, which then interplays with other structural domains like harmony and melody.

   Despite 25 years of study, there's still no comprehensive theory or digital corpus for analyzing forms across styles. Our project aims to fill these gaps. We'll develop a generalized theory of musical form, create a digital corpus with expert analyses, and build computational models for form analysis. 

  This interdisciplinary effort will enhance musicological research and enable applications in Music Information Retrieval and automatic music generation.

Motivation:

   Over the past ca. 25 years, the study of musical form has developed into a vibrant field. So far, most research endeavors have concentrated on describing a small number of prominent, concrete forms focusing on confined historical repertoires. At present, the field still lacks an overarching theory that:

  • models form and its components flexibly across styles 
  • is empirically grounded in large-scale corpus studies 
  • is mathematically precise such that it lends itself for the design of efficient algorithms for computational models of form.

    Further, there is still no large digital corpus of expert form analyses and no computational model achieving high-quality form analyses to aid researchers and musicians. 
 
   The chief purpose of our interdisciplinary project is to address these lacunae.

Goals:

   Firstly, we will develop a generalized formal theory of musical form that brings together state-of-the-art music theory with the theory of formal languages in order to devise a formalism and a notation language that characterizes human expert intuition. 
  â€‹Secondly, we will develop an annotation standard for form analyses in digital scores, compile a large corpus with expert analyses, and develop web-based annotation and visualization tools. 
  Thirdly, we will develop computational models of musical form based on corpus analysis, parsing methods, and machine learning heuristics. The formal model poses the major algorithmic challenge of automatic deduction of form. We will tackle these challenges with state-of-the-art methods to achieve tractability of our model while maintaining the expressive power to produce results that are useful for humans.
